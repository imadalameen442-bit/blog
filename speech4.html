Argument Speech

The Uneven Playing Field

CRS 325 · Spring 2026 · Imad Al-Ameen

Setting the Stage

My argument speech, The Difficulties of Recruitment, took on one of the most quietly accepted inequalities in American sports: college athletic recruiting. On its surface, the process looks like a pure meritocracy: work hard, play well, get noticed. But the moment you look at the actual numbers, that story falls apart. Of the 8 million high school student athletes competing in the United States every year, only about 560,000 NCAA roster spots exist — and less than 2% of those athletes ever reach Division I. That gap between perception and reality was the Kairos of this speech.

The exigence, the reason now is the right moment to make this argument, is that the recruiting landscape has changed dramatically in just the last few years. NIL legislation passed in 2021 opened the door to agents, attorneys, and brand contracts that were once reserved for professionals. Private training costs have skyrocketed. Invite-only showcases have become the standard gateway to visibility. All of this has compounded the financial barrier that was already pricing out talented athletes from lower-income families. This is not a new problem, but it is a louder and more pressing one, and that is exactly why I chose it as my topic.

Argument Speech Reflection

Watching my recording back was a humbling experience, but also a useful one. Let me start with delivery, because that is where the most concrete feedback lives. At the 2:15 mark, I attempted to pivot from my introduction into my first main point, the statistical odds against student athletes, and the transition fell flat. There was no bridging language, no signpost, just a hard cut from one idea to the next. Then at 4:40, the same problem surfaced again as I moved into the financial barriers section. My professor's rubric gave me a 1 out of 5 for transitions, internal previews, and signposts, and honestly, looking at those two moments alone, I understand exactly why.

The filler word situation was also something I needed to hear. I used "furthermore" a few too many times throughout the speech, so many times that it stopped functioning as a transition and started becoming background noise. I also said "uh" at one point, which is something I had been working on since Speech 1. At 2:45, I visibly struggled to find the right word in the moment, and you can hear it in the recording. These are the kinds of things that are very hard to catch in real time but impossible to miss on playback. My professor also pointed out two specific word choice issues: I used "tiny elite" which sounds oddly literal — as if I'm referring to their physical size — and I said "what this can result is" when it should be "what this can result in." Those are small errors, but in an argument speech where you are trying to project credibility, precision matters.

In terms of content, I leaned heavily on logos throughout the speech, which was a deliberate strategy. I opened with the statistic that only 1 in 57 high school athletes ever make it to any college roster, and then immediately followed with the breakdown: 8 million high school athletes, 560,000 NCAA spots, less than 2% reaching D1. The goal was to anchor the entire argument in numbers that are hard to dismiss. I also cited the finding that 68.8% of college recruiting pipelines disproportionately benefit affluent white student athletes — a statistic that took the argument from "this is hard" to "this is structurally unfair." I used ethos by grounding the speech in credible sourcing and a pattern of evidence, and I tried to layer in pathos when discussing the verbal commitment process — specifically the scenario where a recruiter gives a verbal offer and then pulls it back when someone slightly more convenient comes along. That scenario is genuinely crushing, and I wanted the audience to feel the weight of it.

My counterargument section addressed the most common pushback: that the NCAA itself states there are over 500,000 college athlete spots, and that is more than enough. My rebuttal was straightforward — when you look at the actual breakdown, the vast majority of those spots are D3, not D1, and international athletes fill a significant portion of the remaining spots. So the competition is not just 8 million American athletes; it extends globally. The math does not improve when you account for that. I also noted that the argument that "recruited athletes get a big admissions advantage" is only partially true, it applies mainly to athletes at the very top of the pipeline, not the average student athlete grinding through the process. I think this section came through clearly, and the rubric score for counterarguments (4.5 out of 5) reflected that.

I want to be honest about the preparation side of things. That week was genuinely difficult at home — my mom had multiple medical appointments, and between keeping up with school and being present for my family, my practice time for this speech was cut short. That is not an excuse, because it should never be an excuse. But it does explain why the delivery felt "a little choppy," as my professor noted. The main points of this speech were solid. The argument held. What it needed was more repetition. For context on how complex and expensive this system has become, this NCSA Sports overview of the recruiting process lays out exactly the kind of year-by-year grind I was describing, and this breakdown of recruiting costs from STACK quantifies the financial burden in a way that supports the core of my argument.

"The talent does not take the showcase, but the money does. And until we fix that, the playing field will never actually be level."

My goal going into this speech was to make a data-driven, credible argument that held up under scrutiny. I feel I delivered on that, even if the delivery and transitions let the content down. Looking ahead to the epideictic speech, I am setting one focused, achievable goal: to build my transitions into the speech so deeply that they feel invisible. Not "furthermore" dropped in as a placeholder, but actual bridging language that carries the audience from one idea to the next without them ever feeling a bump. If I can make the structure feel effortless, the rest of the speech has room to breathe.
