Persuasive Speech Monroe's Motivated Sequence

The Future of Athletic Recruiting

CRS 325 · Spring 2026 · Imad Al-Ameen

For my third speech, I argued for something I genuinely believe in — RecCamp, a platform I'm building to fix the fragmented, exhausting recruiting process that student athletes deal with every day. Using Monroe's Motivated Sequence, I walked the audience through the problem, the solution, and what the future could look like. Here's my reflection on how it went.

Reflection

Q 1

What was your attention getter?

I opened with a statistic I knew would stop people mid-thought. At 0:08 I said: "Did you know that the NCAA has stated that only 7% of high school athletes make it to college sports — and of those, only 2% reach Division I?" I chose this because it immediately frames the stakes. If the odds are that slim, the recruiting process needs to work flawlessly — and right now, it doesn't. It was the perfect setup for everything that followed.

Q 2

Did you use a transition between your introduction and the body?

Yes — at 2:00, after delivering my preview statement, I used the phrase "to start off" to signal the move into my first main point. It was intentional, though I'll admit it could have been a stronger, more polished pivot. It got the job done, and the audience clearly followed the shift, but it's something I want to sharpen in future speeches.

Q 3

Did you make at least four separate citations? How did you introduce each?

Yes, I cited four sources, and I made a point of introducing each one differently so the speech wouldn't feel like a citation dump. For the document I wrote myself — a detailed log of RecCamp's features and functionality — I first explained why I created it and who it was built for before getting into its content. For the RecCamp Survey, I used natural language like "surveyors have found" or "those surveyed have said." For the Sports Tech Journal and the NCAA statistic, I kept it clean and authoritative: "according to the Sports Tech Journal..." and "the NCAA states..." Varying those introductions made the evidence feel integrated into the argument rather than bolted on.

Q 4

What were your main points, and what words did you use to transition between them?

My three main points followed Monroe's Motivated Sequence. The first was the Need Step — laying out the real problems student athletes face: too many fragmented apps, invisibility to college coaches unless you're already well-known, and constant communication noise from missed emails and disorganized stats. The second was the Satisfaction Step — showing exactly how RecCamp solves all of that through centralization, verified performance stats, AI-driven recruiter matching, and direct messaging. The third was the Visualization Step — asking the audience to picture both the current broken reality and what it looks like when RecCamp is in the picture.

For transitions I used phrases like "furthermore," "in addition to," and "on to my next point." I used review statements on two of my three main points, which helped the audience track where we were. I'll be honest though — I didn't do a strong job incorporating preview statements going into each new section, and that's one of my main goals to fix going forward.

Q 5

What words did you use to signal the end of your speech?

I said "Before I end..." to cue the conclusion. From there I moved into a brief summary of the broken systems RecCamp replaces, issued a call to action for the audience to sign up for the beta launch, and closed with what I think is the strongest line I've written for a speech:

"Your sport is played on the field, but your future is built on how you connect. Let RecCamp be that connection."

Q 6

Describe your use of persuasive techniques. Provide actual words with timestamps.

I used all three classical appeals, and I think the balance between them is what made the speech feel well-rounded rather than one-dimensional.

For Logos, I led with hard data from the very start. At 0:18 I cited the NCAA's finding that only 2% of high school athletes reach Division I — a number that's impossible to argue with and immediately grounds the problem in reality. I continued weaving in evidence throughout all three main points.

For Ethos, I established credibility by making clear I'm not just someone talking about a problem from the outside — I'm building the solution. Around 0:50 I walked the audience through my background as RecCamp's founder, which gave me the authority to speak on what's broken in the current system and why my approach addresses it.

For Pathos, I leaned in during the Visualization Step at 7:10, asking the audience to actually picture what it feels like to be an overlooked athlete — stats buried where no recruiter can find them, DMs going unanswered, expensive niche services that price out rural or under-resourced athletes. Then I flipped it: what does that same athlete's experience look like with RecCamp? And I closed at 8:12 with the line above, which was the emotional anchor for everything that came before it.

Q 7

Describe your energy level. What will you need to address in your argument speech?

My energy was genuinely strong — I was passionate about the topic, and I think that came through. When you're talking about something you've invested real time and thought into building, speaking with conviction comes naturally. That said, looking back I could afford to vary my delivery more. Smiling a bit more at certain moments, shifting my pace and tone between points — those small changes would make the speech feel even more dynamic. For the argument speech, I want to be intentional about those shifts rather than staying at one consistent energy level for the full eight minutes.

Q 8

Describe your nonverbal communication.

This is the area that needs the most work. During the preview statement I used my fingers to physically enumerate my main points — that was deliberate and it worked well. But at other moments I caught myself lightly banging on the podium out of passion, which is exactly the kind of unintentional movement I need to eliminate. Nonverbal communication only works when it's purposeful. Every gesture should reinforce a specific point, not just vent energy. I need to channel that passion into precise, controlled signals.

Q 9

In what ways was your performance different from your informative speech?

The biggest difference was intentionality around flow. In the informative speech I was focused on transferring information clearly. Here, I was focused on leaving the audience with something — a feeling, a perspective, a reason to act. I put a lot more thought into signposts and making sure the audience always knew where we were in the argument. Watching it back, the speech feels more cohesive and more purposeful than my previous one. I also tried much harder to truly leave a memorable message, rather than just covering my points and finishing.

Q 10a

What specifically accounts for the difference?

A few things came together. First, I stuttered noticeably less — I think that's a combination of more practice and genuine comfort with the material. I could actually feel the audience's attention during certain sections, and that feedback loop kept me grounded. Second, I was more strategic about structure this time. I wasn't just thinking about what to say; I was thinking about how the speech would feel as a whole arc. And third, I reached out for help where I needed it. My introduction wasn't landing the way I wanted, so I went directly to Professor Raterman. Getting that outside perspective made a real difference in how I opened the speech, and I'm glad I didn't try to just push through it on my own.

Q 10b

What goal did you set for this speech? Set one small, achievable goal for the next.

My goal was to be more intentional with signposts and create a smoother overall flow — and I genuinely feel like I delivered on that compared to my previous speeches. The structure felt tighter, the transitions were cleaner, and I was more aware of where I was in the speech at any given moment.

For the argument speech, my goal is specifically to get better at preview statements. I used review statements after two of my three main points, but I didn't consistently preview what was coming before I dove in. That one adjustment would make my structure feel even more polished and easier for the audience to follow.

Speech Outline

Title: The Future of Athletic Recruiting

Specific Purpose: By the end of this speech, we will all agree that RecCamp is the essential, all-in-one solution for student-athlete recruiting.

Thesis: By unifying performance, communication, and recruiter access, RecCamp removes app fatigue and boosts athlete visibility.

I. Introduction — Attention Step
  A. Statistics: 7% of athletes reach college sports; 2% reach NCAA D1      (National Collegiate Athletic Association)
  B. Credibility: Founder of RecCamp; firsthand witness to recruiting inefficiencies      (Al-Ameen)
  C. Problem: The process is "way more difficult than it needs to be."      (RecCamp Survey)
  D. Thesis: Unify performance, communication, and recruiter access.
  E. Preview: Chaos of tools → RecCamp solution → streamlined success.

II. Main Point 1 — Need Step
  A. Fragmentation: "Juggling too many different apps and websites at once."      (RecCamp Survey)
  B. Invisibility: "Athletes don't get seen by college coaches unless they're already well-known."      (RecCamp Survey)
  C. Disconnect: Missed emails; disorganized stats; communication noise.

III. Main Point 2 — Satisfaction Step
  A. Centralization: An all-in-one platform — dashboard, communication, marketplace.      (Sports Tech Journal)
  B. Integration: Verified stats; Garmin/Strava sync; real-time performance tracking.      (Al-Ameen)
  C. Efficiency: Recruiter filters; AI-driven matching; direct messaging.

IV. Main Point 3 — Visualization Step
  A. Current Reality: Buried stats; unanswered DMs; expensive niche services.
  B. RecCamp Reality: Automatic updates; instant notifications; exposure for rural athletes.
  C. Ideal Outcome: "Change outcomes for athletes at schools like ours."      (RecCamp Survey)

V. Conclusion — Action Step
  A. Summary: Broken systems → RecCamp fix → leveled playing field.
  B. Call to Action: Sign up; beta launch; join the movement.
  C. Closing: "Your sport is played on the field, but your future is built on how you connect. Let RecCamp be that connection."
