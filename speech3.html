Persuasive Speech Reflection

My speech, The Future of Athletic Recruiting, was a persuasive argument for RecCamp, a platform I am personally building to fix the fragmented, exhausting recruiting process that student athletes go through every day. Using Monroe's Motivated Sequence, I walked the audience through the problem, the solution, and a vision of what recruiting could actually look like when it works the way it should.

I opened with an attention getter I knew would stop people mid-thought. At 0:08 I said: "Did you know that the NCAA has stated that only 7% of high school athletes make it to college sports, and of those, only 2% reach Division I?" I chose this because it immediately frames the stakes. If the odds are already that slim, the recruiting process needs to work perfectly, and right now it does not. After delivering my preview statement, I used the phrase "to start off" at 2:00 to signal the beginning of my first main point. It was intentional, and while it got the job done, I know I can make that kind of transition stronger and more polished going forward.

My three main points followed Monroe's Motivated Sequence. The first was the Need Step, where I established the real problems student athletes are dealing with: too many fragmented apps, invisibility to college coaches unless you are already well-known, and constant communication noise from missed emails and disorganized stats. The second was the Satisfaction Step, where I showed exactly how RecCamp solves all of that through centralization, verified performance stats, AI-driven recruiter matching, and direct messaging. The third was the Visualization Step, where I asked the audience to picture both the current broken reality and what life looks like with RecCamp in the picture. For transitions I used phrases like "furthermore," "in addition to," and "on to my next point." I used review statements on two of my three main points, which helped the audience keep track of where we were. I will be honest though, I did not do a strong job incorporating preview statements going into each new section, and that is something I want to work on. To signal the end of the speech, I said "Before I end..." and moved into my conclusion from there.

I cited four sources throughout the speech and made sure to introduce each one differently. For the document I personally wrote about RecCamp's features and functionality, I first explained why I created it and who it was built for before getting into its contents. For the RecCamp Survey, I used phrases like "surveyors have said" or "those surveyed have found." For the Sports Tech Journal and the NCAA statistic, I kept it straightforward: "according to the Sports Tech Journal" or "the NCAA states." Varying those introductions made the evidence feel integrated into the argument rather than just tacked on at random.

When it came to persuasion, I used all three classical appeals. For Logos, I led with data right from the start. At 0:18 I cited the NCAA's finding that only 2% of athletes reach Division I, a number that immediately grounds the whole argument in reality. For Ethos, around 0:50 I established my credibility by making clear I am not just someone speaking about a problem from the outside. I am building the solution. Being RecCamp's founder gave me the authority to speak on what is broken in the current system and why my approach addresses it. For Pathos, I leaned in during the Visualization Step at 7:10, asking the audience to really picture what it feels like to be an overlooked athlete with buried stats and unanswered messages, priced out of expensive niche services. I closed at 8:12 with what I think is the strongest line I have written for a speech:

"Your sport is played on the field, but your future is built on how you connect. Let RecCamp be that connection."

My energy level was genuinely strong throughout. When you are talking about something you have invested real time and thought into building, speaking with conviction comes naturally. Looking back though, I think I could vary my delivery a bit more. Smiling at certain moments, shifting my pace between points here and there would make the speech feel even more dynamic. That is something I want to be more intentional about heading into the argument speech.

Nonverbal communication is the area that needs the most work. During the preview statement I used my fingers to enumerate my main points, which was deliberate and worked well. But at other moments I caught myself lightly banging on the podium out of passion, which is exactly the kind of unintentional movement I need to eliminate. Every gesture needs to be purposeful. I cannot do wasted movement, and I need to channel that energy into precise, controlled signals instead.

Compared to my informative speech, the biggest difference in this one was how focused I was on the overall flow and truly leaving the audience with something to remember, rather than just delivering information and wrapping up. I tried much harder to use signposts consistently and make sure the audience always knew where we were in the argument. I also stuttered noticeably less, which I think came from both more practice and genuine comfort with the material. I was more strategic about structure this time too, and I made a point of reaching out for help where I needed it. My introduction was not landing the way I wanted, so I went directly to Professor Raterman. Getting that outside perspective made a real difference in how I opened the speech, and I am glad I did not just push through it on my own.

My goal coming into this speech was to have a better use of signposts and a more flowing overall delivery, and I genuinely feel like I delivered on that compared to my previous speeches. For my next speech, I want to focus specifically on preview statements. I used review statements after two of my three main points, but I did not consistently preview what was coming before diving into each new section. That one adjustment would make the structure feel even tighter. If you want to understand why a unified platform like RecCamp matters, it helps to see what tools like NCSA Sports currently offer student athletes and where the gaps still are. RecCamp aims to go further.
